Liquid Treatment Options

Liquid termiticides are a common and highly effective method for creating a protective barrier around your property. These products are applied to the soil surrounding your foundation, creating a treated zone that termites cannot cross. When termites attempt to tunnel through the treated soil, they are exposed to the active ingredient, which is lethal. Newer generation liquid termiticides are non-repellent, meaning termites cannot detect them and therefore unknowingly come into contact with the product. This allows the termiticide to be transferred throughout the colony, leading to a more complete elimination. We carefully and strategically apply these products to form a continuous, chemical barrier that deters termites from entering your home. Termite Baiting Systems

Termite baiting systems offer an alternative and often environmentally friendly approach to termite control. These systems utilize bait stations strategically placed around the perimeter of your property and in areas of known termite activity. The bait stations contain cellulose material that termites readily consume. This cellulose is laced with an insect growth regulator or slow-acting toxicant. Termites consume the bait and carry it back to their colony, where it is shared with other termites. The active ingredient disrupts the termites' growth or development, eventually leading to the decline and collapse of the entire colony. Baiting systems offer a less disruptive treatment method and can be highly effective in eliminating the colony at its source. The monitoring and replenishment of bait stations are crucial for the success of this method, and we provide ongoing service to ensure the system remains effective.

Recognizing the Signs of a Termite Infestation

Being able to identify the early warning signs of a termite infestation is crucial for preventing extensive damage to your property. Termites often work silently and hidden from view, making detection challenging for the untrained eye. However, understanding the common indicators can help you spot a problem before it escalates. If you suspect you might have termites, contacting a professional like Kankakee Pest Pros for a thorough inspection is the best course of action. Delaying action can lead to more significant damage and higher repair costs. Let's explore some of the key signs to watch out for.

Mud Tubes

Subterranean termites build mud tubes, also known as shelter tubes, along surfaces to travel between their underground colony and their food source (your home's wooden structures). These tubes are made of soil, wood particles, and termite saliva. They are typically found on foundation walls, crawl space supports, or any other surface that connects the ground to porous materials like wood. These tubes protect the termites from predators and dehydration as they travel. Finding mud tubes, even if they appear dry or abandoned, is a strong indicator of past or present subterranean termite activity and warrants immediate investigation. It's like a highway for termites, a clear sign they've been there or are currently active.

Damaged Wood and Wood Dust (Frass)

While termites consume wood, they don't always leave obvious external signs of damage until the infestation is significant. However, if you tap on wood surfaces and they sound hollow, it could be a sign that termites have been eating the interior. Drywood termites, unlike subterranean termites, don't require contact with soil and will nest and feed directly within the wood itself. They push out waste material called frass, which resembles sawdust or small pellets, from small holes in the wood. Accumulations of frass near wooden structures are a strong indicator of a drywood termite infestation. Inspecting areas like attics, furniture, and wooden structural elements is important for detecting this sign. Concerned about other wood-damaging pests? Swarming Termites and Discarded Wings

Termites reproduce by sending out ""swarmers,"" which are winged reproductive termites that leave the colony to establish new ones. Swarming typically occurs during specific times of the year, often after rain, and can happen both indoors and outdoors. The presence of swarming termites around your property, particularly inside your home, is a definitive sign of an active termite infestation. After swarming, the termites shed their wings. Finding piles of small, translucent wings near windowsills, doorways, or light sources is another strong indication that termites have been swarming from a colony within or near your home. Don't dismiss discarded wings as just random insect activity; they are a clear warning sign that you need termite treatment.

Bubbling or Peeling Paint

Sometimes, a termite infestation can be mistaken for water damage. Termite activity within walls can cause paint or wallpaper to bubble or peel as they tunnel through the wood and introduce moisture. This is often seen in areas with high humidity or where termites are actively feeding close to the surface. While bubbling paint can have other causes, if it occurs in conjunction with other potential termite signs, it is worth investigating further. A professional inspection can help distinguish between termite damage and other issues.
